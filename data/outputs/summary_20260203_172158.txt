## Research Review: The Macroeconomic Impacts of the 9/11 Attack

This review synthesizes the findings of the research paper "The Macroeconomic Impacts of the 9/11 Attack: Evidence from Real-Time Forecasting," authored by Bryan W. Roberts (August 2009).

### 1. Abstract

This review synthesizes the findings of Roberts (2009) regarding the macroeconomic impacts of the 9/11 attacks. The study employs real-time forecast revision analysis to isolate and quantify the immediate shock. Findings indicate a 0.5 percentage point reduction in 2001 Real GDP growth and a 0.11 percentage point increase in the unemployment rate (equivalent to 598,000 jobs lost). Crucially, forecasters anticipated a divergence: GDP forecasts recovered rapidly, whereas unemployment forecasts remained persistently elevated, effectively predicting the 2002 "jobless recovery." This methodology is validated as an effective tool for quantifying sudden catastrophic shocks.

### 2. Methods Comparison

The methodological analysis focuses on the unified approach employed by Roberts (2009) to quantify the economic shock of the September 11, 2001, terrorist attacks.

#### Core Technique: Real-Time Forecast Revision Analysis

The study utilizes a **real-time forecasting** methodology specifically designed to isolate the anticipated economic impact of a sudden, catastrophic event. This technique, known as **Forecast Revision Analysis**, compares professional macroeconomic forecasts issued immediately *prior* to 9/11 (establishing the baseline) with those issued immediately *after* the attack.

**Rationale and Scope:**
The rationale is that this comparison captures the instantaneous incorporation of the 9/11 shock into the economic models and judgmental assessments of expert forecasters. Analyzing these revisions allows the quantification of the shock independent of subsequent policy responses or post-hoc data adjustments. The scope covers both the short-run (2001 outcomes) and the medium-run (2002 outcomes) for U.S. real GDP growth and the unemployment rate.

**Robustness and Framework:**
A robustness check is included to ensure that the observed forecast changes exceed typical fluctuations over the forecasting horizon, even during recessionary periods. Theoretically, the paper posits that accurate disaster assessment must account for two complex, endogenous factors:
1.  **Mitigation Responses:** Actions taken by the private and public sectors (e.g., business relocation, government aid).
2.  **Behavioral Changes:** The influence of the "fear factor," which alters consumer and investor behavior by increasing the perceived probability of future attacks.

### 3. Results Synthesis

The findings provide specific quantitative estimates for the immediate impact of the 9/11 shock and qualitative observations regarding the anticipated recovery path for the subsequent year.

#### Real GDP Growth
The immediate impact on U.S. Real GDP growth for 2001 was quantified as a significant negative shock: a **0.5 percentage point reduction** in the annual growth rate.

For the medium term (2002), the GDP growth forecast initially dropped dramatically following 9/11 but **recovered fully** later in 2001. This suggested that forecasters anticipated either effective mitigation responses or a temporary shock to output.

#### Unemployment Rate
The impact on the labor market was immediate and substantial. The unemployment rate was forecast to increase by **0.11 percentage points** in 2001, translating to an equivalent loss of **598,000 jobs**.

Crucially, the medium-term forecast for the 2002 unemployment rate rose sharply immediately after 9/11 and **never subsequently returned** to the pre-attack baseline level.

#### Interpretation of Divergence
The most significant finding is the divergence in recovery expectations between output (GDP) and employment. Although forecasters quickly revised output projections upward for 2002, they maintained a sustained high unemployment forecast. The analysis of Roberts (2009) yields four major insights regarding the economic consequences of the 9/11 attack and the methodology used for disaster assessment:
